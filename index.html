Godot

Chess engines have always fascinated me. During the summer of 2012 I decided I would try my hand at making my own chess program. The result is the lovingly-named Godot.

Despite the fact that I'm an awful chess player, Godot turned out to be quite good, far exceeding my expectations. Using some of the techniques employed by the best engines out there, Godot plays a pretty serious game of chess. At one minute chess, it will defeat 99.89% of humans.

I even made a Selenium Webdriver program that connects Godot to players online. Godot reached an impressive 2242 Elo rating online, and along the way defeated a few titled players, including many FIDE-titled CMs, FMs, and WGMs.

I recently entered Godot into the local science fair and I got pretty far, receiving an honorable mention at the California state science fair. I wrote a pretty paper in LaTeX about the program. You can follow the link below for a complete rundown on Godot.

The Musical Turk

Inspired by Animusic's Pipe Dream, the Musical Turk is a WebGL MIDI visualization experiment. It throws marbles that strike notes in sync with music. The result? A pretty awesome 3D interactive experience, right in your browser.

The Musical Turk is in Javascript and relies on three.js for visuals and midi.js for in-browser MIDI playing. One of my favorite parts about Turk is that it adapts to lag from slow internet connection or delays when rendering or playing music, dynamically speeding up or slowing down to have very precise synchronization between the marbles and the sound. Try it for yourself; it's pretty cool.

Note: The Musical Turk only works in Chrome; no other browser can play MIDI right. Sorry about that.

Interlingua

Imagine if you will: a programming language whose keywords are pre-defined by the user for whatever his needs. That's, in essence, what Interlingua is.

I made Interlingua because it had always bothered me that people learning computer science from the non-English-speaking world had to make an awkward compromise between having English-language keywords and native-language variables. I thought Interlingua would be a cute way of trying to fix that. I also used it to make my own flavor of LOLCODE.

I made Interlingua from scratch, and given that it's pretty good. I wrote the lexer, interpreter, and runtime in Ruby; the parser I wrote in RACC. Interlingua has basic object orientation, method overriding, monkey patching, and Python-style indentation. I'm pretty proud of it.
